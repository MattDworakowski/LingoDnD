# LingoDnD — Tone & Style Guide (German narration)

> The single most important authoring document. Tone failed **twice** in
> predecessor playtests — once "too cute / too baby", once "too dark / too
> spooky". This guide pins down the narrow target in the middle and gives concrete
> rules + examples so every episode lands in the same place.

## The target in one line
**A classic high-fantasy adventure told warmly to a brave young hero — real
stakes, real courage, never grim, never saccharine.** Think *The Hobbit* read
aloud to a 7-year-old, in slow, simple German.

---

## Voice & language rules

1. **Second person, present tense.** "Du gehst in den dunklen Wald." Never third
   person, never a named child — this keeps the audio gender-neutral (see BRD §8.4)
   and pulls the listener *into* the story.
2. **One idea per sentence. Short sentences.** A 6–9-year-old learner parses
   "Vor dir liegt ein großer Wald. Der Weg ist dunkel." far better than one long
   subordinate-clause sentence.
3. **Common, concrete vocabulary.** Prefer everyday words; introduce a *few*
   fantasy words on purpose (Elf, Zwerg, Zauber, Sternstein) and let context carry
   them. New words are a feature — but rationed.
4. **Slow pace.** Written to be read at the TTS "slow" setting. Avoid tongue-
   twisters and dense consonant clusters.
5. **Repetition is good pedagogy.** Recurring phrases ("Du bist tapfer.", "Der Weg
   führt weiter.") aid comprehension and feel storybook-rhythmic.
6. **Address courage, not fear.** The hero is capable. Danger is framed as
   *exciting and surmountable*, helpers appear, and **failure always still moves
   the story forward** (no dead ends, no "Game Over").

---

## The three zones (calibrate against these)

Same beat — meeting a goblin on the path — written three ways:

| ❌ Too cute / baby | ✅ Just right | ❌ Too dark / scary |
|---|---|---|
| "Oh nein! Ein klitzekleiner, süßer Kobold mit einem winzigen Knüppelchen hüpft fröhlich auf den Weg. Hihi, wie niedlich!" | "Plötzlich springt ein Kobold auf den Weg. 'Niemand kommt hier vorbei!', kreischt er und schwingt seinen Knüppel." | "Eine grauenhafte Kreatur mit blutigen Zähnen stürzt sich auf dich und will dich zerfleischen." |

What makes the middle column right:
- The danger is **real** (he blocks the path, he's armed) → not babyish.
- It's **not graphic or terrifying** (no blood, no death, no helplessness) → not dark.
- A touch of **character/humor** is allowed (the kobold is grumpy, a bit comical),
  but it doesn't undercut the stakes.

---

## Do / Don't

**Do**
- Use light humor for balance — bickering elves and dwarves, a grumpy goblin, a
  chatty forest bird.
- Give the hero warm, quiet beats (a campfire, sharing bread) between the action.
  These beats are where "not too dark" is *proven*, and they're great listening
  practice.
- Let helpers and friends appear, especially after a failed roll.
- Keep peril physical and external (a broken bridge, a blocked path, a rival),
  resolvable by cleverness, courage, strength, magic, or charm.

**Don't**
- Don't pile up diminutives (*-chen / -lein*) or "süß / niedlich / klitzeklein".
  One is charm; five is baby talk.
- Don't use death, blood, gore, torture, true horror, or hopeless/abandoned moods.
- Don't make the hero a passive victim — they always have an option.
- Don't moralize or talk down ("Und das war eine wichtige Lektion, nicht wahr?").

---

## Fantasy register cheat-sheet
Recurring world words to reuse (consistency = comprehension): **Aldoria** (das
Reich), **der Sternstein**, **Elf / Elfe**, **Zwerg**, **der Magier**, **der
Zauber / zaubern**, **der Held / die Heldin** (only on-screen, not in audio),
**das Abenteuer**, **die Spur**, **tapfer**, **mutig**.
